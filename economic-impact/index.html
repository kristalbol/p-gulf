Economic Snapshot — Day 49

Day 49 delivers the war's most dramatic economic reversal. Iran's reopening of the Strait of Hormuz sent oil prices plunging 10-12% — the biggest single-day drop since the conflict began — with WTI falling to $93.74 and Brent to $88.73. Equities surged to all-time highs: S&P 500 at 7,128 (+1.23%), Nasdaq at 24,103, Dow up 1,005 points. The VIX dropped below 18 for the first time in weeks. Yet beneath the market euphoria lies a deeply stressed real economy: University of Michigan consumer sentiment hit 47.6 — the lowest reading in the survey's 74-year history — while one-year inflation expectations surged to 4.8%, up a full percentage point from March.

The divergence between financial markets and consumer sentiment is the defining economic story of Day 49. Wall Street prices in peace; Main Street still feels the damage from 7 weeks of disrupted supply chains, elevated energy costs, and food price inflation. Fertilizer prices remain 23-49% above pre-war levels depending on the product, with urea spiking $230/ton in 6 weeks — a crisis that will flow through to food prices for months regardless of ceasefire outcomes. The FAO Food Price Index rose 2.4% to 128.5 in March. Defense stocks have pulled back slightly (LMT -1.6% today to $598) as peace optimism erodes war premiums. The Dollar Index weakened to 97.70 as safe-haven demand eases. Gold climbed to $4,867 — a mixed signal between lingering uncertainty and inflation hedging. The ISM Manufacturing PMI at 52.7 suggests factory activity remains expansionary, but the consumer confidence collapse signals demand destruction ahead. This is an economy where the ceasefire has healed the price of oil but not yet the price of groceries, confidence, or supply chain resilience.

Key Economic Divergences

Markets vs. Main Street: S&P 500 at all-time highs while consumer sentiment is at 74-year lows. This disconnect reflects that financial markets are forward-looking (pricing in peace), while consumer surveys capture accumulated pain (7 weeks of inflation, uncertainty, supply disruption).

Oil vs. Food: Oil has dropped ~37% from war-peak ~$150 to $93, but fertilizer prices remain 23-49% elevated. The agricultural supply chain has multi-month lag times — even full Hormuz normalization won't reverse food inflation for 3-6 months.

VIX vs. Inflation Expectations: VIX at 17.28 signals calm; inflation expectations at 4.8% signal alarm. Markets see volatility easing but consumers see prices rising — both can be correct simultaneously.

Defense Stocks vs. Broad Market: LMT, RTX, NOC pulling back as peace optimism rises. Defense sector outperformed by ~30% since Feb 28 but is now giving back gains. War premium unwinding = peace dividend redistributing to growth sectors.

Hormuz Strait — The Oil Chokepoint

Normal flow: ~21 million barrels/day (~20% of global oil supply), plus ~25% of global LNG

Day 49 status: Iran declared Hormuz "completely open" for commercial vessels. Ships must use Iran-coordinated routes via Iran Ports & Maritime Organisation. US maintains blockade targeting Iran-bound/Iran-origin ships only. Dual status: open for global commerce, blocked for Iranian trade.

Oil price math: Peak disruption removed ~4-5M bbl/day from markets → $150 oil. Hormuz reopening restores bulk of transit → $88-94 oil. Full normalization (incl. Iran trade) would push toward $75-80. US blockade on Iran keeps ~2M bbl/day constrained.

Consumer impact: US gasoline peaked ~$5.50/gal, now trending toward $4.00. Full normalization target: $3.50-3.80. Every $10/bbl oil = ~$0.25/gal gasoline. Airlines: fuel surcharges of $50-150/ticket remain in effect.

Trade Impact Assessment

Strait of Hormuz: Iran's reopening has restored most commercial transit, but US enforcement of the Iran blockade creates a two-tier system. Ships transiting must use Iran-coordinated routes. Insurance premiums remain at 5x pre-war levels — insurers lag ceasefire optimism by weeks. Full shipping cost normalization requires sustained peace (est. 4-8 weeks post-deal).

WTO downgrade: Global merchandise trade growth slashed from 4.6% to 1.9% for 2026. High-energy scenario (escalation): 1.4%. The war removed ~$800B in projected global trade. Recovery depends on ceasefire permanence and supply chain re-establishment.

Red Sea compound effect: Houthi disruptions (since late 2023) + Hormuz crisis = double chokepoint pressure. Suez Canal traffic already down 60% from Houthi attacks. Ships rerouting around Africa add 10-14 days and $1M+ per voyage. Hormuz reopening helps, but Red Sea remains problematic.

Consumer pass-through: Elevated shipping costs → 2-4% higher consumer goods prices within 60-90 days. Even with oil falling, the shipping/logistics surcharge embedded in product prices will take 3-6 months to unwind.
