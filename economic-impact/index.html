Economic Snapshot — Day 52

Day 52 — the seizure. The US Navy fired on and captured the Iranian-flagged cargo ship M/V Touska after a six-hour standoff in the Gulf of Oman. The USS Spruance disabled the 900-foot vessel's propulsion, and Marines from the 31st MEU rappelled aboard. It's the first non-military Iranian ship hit during the war and the first captured since the blockade began. Iran immediately called it "piracy" and a ceasefire violation, vowing military retaliation. Markets responded: oil surged 5.3% (WTI $87.88, Brent $95.62), S&P 500 futures fell 0.7%, European indices dropped sharply (DAX -1.6%, FTSE -0.6%). The Touska seizure transforms the blockade from an economic measure into active naval warfare — every Iranian vessel is now a potential target, and every US ship is now a potential retaliation target. The ceasefire, which both sides accuse each other of violating, expires Wednesday.

The diplomatic picture has collapsed. Iran's foreign ministry declared Monday: "As of now, we have no plans for the next round of negotiations." Vance, Witkoff, and Kushner are in Pakistan but Tehran isn't coming. Iran's demands — frozen assets, war reparations, Hormuz control, regional ceasefire including Lebanon — are maximalist. The US demands — nuclear commitment, Hormuz reopening — are equally non-negotiable. With 2 days until ceasefire expiry and zero negotiating framework, the economic outlook darkens sharply. Oil at $87.88 is just the beginning — if ceasefire lapses Wednesday, $100+ is near-certain. Defense stocks surging (LMT $592, RTX $196). Consumer sentiment at 74-year low will deteriorate further. The Touska seizure adds a new dimension: naval confrontation escalation. Iran has promised retaliation — if they target a US vessel or allied shipping, we enter a new phase entirely. Phase classification: "CEASEFIRE COLLAPSE — Active Naval Confrontation."
