Economic Snapshot — Day 54

Day 54 — the whipsaw. Two massive developments in 12 hours: Trump extended the ceasefire indefinitely Tuesday evening, saying he'd wait for a "unified proposal" from Iran's "seriously fractured" government. Markets rallied overnight — S&P futures +0.76%, oil initially dipped. Then at ~10am Wednesday, Iran's IRGC seized two container ships in the Strait of Hormuz (MSC Francesca and Epaminondas) and fired on a third Greek-linked vessel, claiming "maritime violations." It's the Touska retaliation. Brent crude spiked back above $100 for the first time since mid-March. Markets gave back most ceasefire-extension gains. The economic picture is now defined by CONTRADICTION: ceasefire extended (positive) but Iran demonstrating it can seize ships at will (devastating for shipping, insurance, trade). A 30-nation coalition is forming at a Royal Air Force base north of London to create a multinational naval mission to safeguard Hormuz — unprecedented in scale.

Key Economic Divergences

Ceasefire Extension Paradox: Trump's indefinite extension should be the most bullish signal since the war began — no bombing, open-ended diplomacy. But Iran's same-day ship seizures obliterate the peace premium. The market is pricing BOTH outcomes simultaneously: ceasefire (no bombing, equities recover) AND continued naval warfare (ship seizures, Hormuz closed, oil $100+). This contradiction is unprecedented — a ceasefire where both sides are actively seizing each other's ships. The result: extreme volatility with no directional conviction.

Brent Breaches $100: The psychologically critical $100 level was breached Wednesday when IRGC seized the MSC Francesca and Epaminondas. WTI closed Tuesday at $92.13 (+2.81%), Brent at $98.48 (+3.14%), and the ship seizures pushed Brent past $100 intraday. The ceasefire extension should have pushed oil DOWN — instead, Iran proved that no ship is safe in Hormuz regardless of ceasefire status. Oil is now pricing permanent disruption, not temporary conflict. $100 Brent may become the new floor.

30-Nation Coalition — Hormuz Securitization: Military planners from 30+ countries meeting at RAF base to form multinational naval mission for Hormuz. This is the international community acknowledging that Hormuz is a permanent crisis zone requiring military escort for commercial shipping. The economic implications: war risk insurance may normalize (if coalition provides escort), but transit costs rise permanently (military escort fees, delays, routing constraints). This is NOT a return to normal — it's institutionalized disruption.

Iran's Retaliation — Tit-for-Tat: The IRGC ship seizures are textbook tit-for-tat retaliation for the Touska. US seizes Iranian ship → Iran seizes two ships + fires on a third. The message: Iran can match US actions ship-for-ship. This is the worst possible outcome for shipping economics — BOTH sides now seizing vessels in Hormuz. War risk insurance is permanently unquotable. Shipping costs will embed this risk for years. The ceasefire is irrelevant to the naval confrontation — it continues regardless.
